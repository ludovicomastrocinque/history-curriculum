HIST 102

The Ancient Mediterranean: Mesopotamia, Egypt, Greece & Rome

The civilisations of the ancient Mediterranean were not isolated achievements — they were deeply interconnected, mutually influencing, and far more contested than later triumphalist narratives admitted. This course examines four of antiquity's greatest civilisations: Mesopotamia (the cradle of cities and writing), Egypt (the longest-lasting state in human history), Greece (the laboratory of democracy and philosophy), and Rome (the architect of Western law and imperial administration). At every turn, you will encounter scholarly disagreement — about origins, about influence, about who deserves credit, and about whose history is being told.

How This Course Works

This course consists of five modules, each exploring one of the ancient Mediterranean's greatest civilisations. Each module includes:

  • Guiding Questions to frame your thinking
  • Core Readings — a curated selection of books and primary sources
  • Academic Sources for deeper research
  • Online Resources — clickable links to free lectures, podcasts, documentaries, and museum collections
  • Key Debates — scholarly disagreements that remain live and contested
  • Discussion Prompts to sharpen your analysis
  • An Essay Assignment (minimum 800 words) that you submit to Claude for grading

Module N+1 unlocks only after you have submitted the essay for Module N. A final essay (1,500+ words) unlocks after all five modules are complete. This course is designed for serious engagement — not passive consumption. You are expected to read, think, debate, and defend your interpretations.

How to Use the Readings

The Core Readings are central texts you should read in full (or large portions thereof). They are the foundation of this course. The Academic Sources are for deeper research if you want to engage with specific scholarly debates. The Online Resources provide free access to lectures, podcasts, documentaries, and museum collections to supplement your reading.

On Academic Integrity

Your essays should be your own work. You are welcome to discuss ideas with others, consult scholarly sources, and seek feedback. But when you submit your essay to Claude, it should reflect your own thinking and your own words. Claude's role is to provide expert feedback on your arguments, not to generate them for you.

Module 1: Mesopotamia — The First Cities and Empires

Timeframe: c. 3500–539 BCE

Overview

Between the Tigris and Euphrates, humans built the world's first cities, invented writing, codified law, and created the first literature. The Epic of Gilgamesh predates Homer by over a thousand years. But Mesopotamian civilisation also raises hard questions: were these early states cooperative achievements, or were they — as James C. Scott argues in Against the Grain — fundamentally coercive structures built on grain extraction, slavery, and forced labour?

Core Readings

A History of the Ancient Near East ca. 3000–323 BC — Marc Van De Mieroop (4th ed., Wiley, 2024). The leading comprehensive textbook on Mesopotamian history by the Professor of History at Columbia University.
The Epic of Gilgamesh — translated by Andrew George (Penguin Classics, 1999). The world's oldest work of literature (c. 2100 BCE). Read it — not just about it.
Cuneiform: Ancient Scripts — Irving Finkel (Getty Publications, 2012). Finkel is an Assyriologist at the British Museum; this explains the invention and evolution of writing with real tablet examples.

Academic Sources

  • Tuplin, C. "Managing the World: Herodotus on Persian Imperial Administration." In Herodotus and the Persian Empire (2013). Examines how Persian administrative systems evolved from earlier Mesopotamian models.
  • Postgate, J.N. — chapters on early Mesopotamian society, economy, and urban innovation (standard Assyriology reference works).

Key Debate: The Hydraulic Hypothesis — Did Irrigation Create the State?

Position A — Wittfogel's Hydraulic Hypothesis (1957): Large-scale irrigation systems required centralised state control to coordinate labour and water distribution. This led to 'hydraulic empires' — inherently despotic states in Mesopotamia, Egypt, China, and Mesoamerica, where control of water meant control of people. The state was created by environmental necessity.

Position B — The Critique (Robert McCormick Adams Jr. and others): Archaeological excavation of Mesopotamia demonstrates that dynastic authority developed BEFORE large-scale canal systems, not after them. The state was not created by irrigation bureaucracy. Adams's fieldwork showed that early Mesopotamian political organisation was more decentralised and competitive than Wittfogel's model allowed. Modern scholars also note Wittfogel's model was partly shaped by Cold War anti-communist politics (he was drawing a parallel between ancient 'oriental despotism' and Soviet communism).

Discussion Prompts

  1. The Epic of Gilgamesh is the world's oldest surviving work of literature. What does it tell us about what Mesopotamians valued, feared, and believed? Does reading a primary source change your understanding of this civilisation compared to reading about it?
  2. Wittfogel's hydraulic hypothesis has been largely discredited archaeologically, yet it remains influential. Why do grand theories about the origins of the state persist even when the evidence against them is strong?
  3. Writing was invented as an accounting tool — to record grain and livestock transactions. Does the mundane origin of writing change how you think about it as a cultural achievement?

Additional Deep Dive

  • Ancient Iraq — Georges Roux (3rd ed., Routledge, 2002). Comprehensive survey.
  • The Sumerians: Their History, Culture, and Character — Samuel Noah Kramer (University of Chicago Press, 1963).
  • The Ancient Near East: A Very Short Introduction — Amanda Podany (Oxford, 2014).

Module 2: Ancient Egypt — The Nile and Its Civilisations

Timeframe: c. 3100–332 BCE
📚 IMPORTANT NOTE: You already own The Rise and Fall of Ancient Egypt by Toby Wilkinson (Random House, 2010). This is your primary text for this module — make it the foundation of your essay.

Overview

Egypt is the longest-lasting state in human history — over three thousand years. It is also one of the most contested: who were the ancient Egyptians? Were they African? Mediterranean? Something else? The debate, which exploded with the work of Cheikh Anta Diop and later Martin Bernal, goes far beyond academic classification — it is a debate about who owns the past, whose civilisation gets to be the foundation of 'Western' history, and how racism shaped the discipline of Egyptology itself.

Core Readings

The Rise and Fall of Ancient Egypt — Toby Wilkinson (Random House, 2010). [YOU ALREADY OWN THIS — IT IS YOUR PRIMARY TEXT FOR THIS MODULE.] Wilkinson's narrative history covers three millennia of Egyptian civilisation with rigour and accessibility; it places Egypt in its Mediterranean and African context and does not shy from the coercive dimensions of pharaonic power.
The African Origin of Civilization: Myth or Reality — Cheikh Anta Diop (Lawrence Hill Publishers, 1974). ESSENTIAL for understanding the Afrocentric argument. Diop argued that traditional Egyptology was Eurocentric and denied Egypt's African identity. His work remains influential and contested.
The Oxford History of Ancient Egypt — ed. Ian Shaw (Oxford University Press, 2000). The authoritative scholarly survey; use as a reference alongside Wilkinson.

Academic Sources

  • Kamugisha, A. (2003). "Finally in Africa? Egypt, from Diop to Celenko." Race and Class, 45(1). Examines the legacy and scholarly critiques of Diop's work.
  • O'Connor, D. & Reid, A. (eds.), Ancient Egypt in Africa (University of Pennsylvania Press, 2003) — chapters on Egyptian-Nubian interactions and bidirectional cultural exchange.

Key Debate: Was Ancient Egypt African? The Afrocentric Debate

Position A — Afrocentric Position (Cheikh Anta Diop; later Martin Bernal's Black Athena): Ancient Egypt was fundamentally African, with significant sub-Saharan African ancestry and deep cultural ties to the African continent. Traditional Egyptology was shaped by 19th-century Eurocentric racism that systematically 'whitened' the Egyptians to make their civilisation a precursor to Greece and Rome rather than to Africa. The 25th Dynasty (Nubian pharaohs) was not an 'invasion' but a restoration of a fundamentally African civilisation.

Position B — Traditional Egyptology's Response: Egypt's primary cultural, linguistic, and geographic affinities lay with the Near East and the broader Mediterranean world. Nile Valley geography placed Egypt at the crossroads of Africa, Asia, and the Mediterranean — not exclusively within sub-Saharan Africa. The 'race' question is largely anachronistic (ancient Egyptians did not understand race as modern people do).

The Current Scholarly Position: Most modern scholars have moved away from racial categories entirely. What is undisputed: (1) Egypt and Nubia/Kush had profound, bidirectional relationships — not master/subordinate; (2) The 25th Dynasty was real and significant; (3) 19th-century Egyptology WAS shaped by racial assumptions; (4) Ancient DNA studies (2017) are contested and methodologically complex. The debate has productively forced scholars to take African connections seriously.

Discussion Prompts

  1. Wilkinson argues that pharaonic power was far more violent and coercive than the monuments suggest. How does his account complicate the popular image of ancient Egypt as a harmonious civilisation?
  2. The debate between Diop's Afrocentrism and traditional Egyptology is partly about evidence, but partly about politics. Can you separate the two? Should you?
  3. The 25th Dynasty — when Nubian kings from Kush ruled Egypt — is often treated as a footnote in Egyptology textbooks. Why might that be, and what does it tell us about historiography?

Additional Deep Dive

  • Black Athena: The Afroasiatic Roots of Classical Civilization, Vol. I — Martin Bernal (Rutgers, 1987).
  • Red Land, Black Land: Daily Life in Ancient Egypt — Barbara Mertz (2nd ed., Harper Perennial, 2008).
  • The Complete Tutankhamun — Nicholas Reeves (Thames and Hudson, 1990).

Module 3: The Greek World — Democracy, Philosophy, and War

Timeframe: c. 800–323 BCE

Overview

Athens is celebrated as the birthplace of democracy. But Athenian democracy excluded women, enslaved roughly a third of the population, and expelled foreigners from political life. The 'Greek miracle' — the sudden flowering of philosophy, science, and democratic thought — has been used to construct a narrative of Western uniqueness. Scholars like Martin Bernal challenge whether this 'miracle' was as original as advertised, or whether it drew heavily — and unacknowledged — from Egyptian and Near Eastern foundations.

Core Readings

The Athenian Revolution — Josiah Ober (Princeton University Press, 1996). The leading scholarly examination of how Athenian democracy emerged and what it actually meant — including who was excluded.
The Spartans: The World of the Warrior-Heroes of Ancient Greece — Paul Cartledge (Oxford University Press, 2002). Authoritative account by one of the world's leading experts on ancient Greece; a crucial counterpoint to the Athenian narrative.
Black Athena: The Afroasiatic Roots of Classical Civilization, Vol. I — Martin Bernal (Rutgers University Press, 1987). Provocative argument that Greek civilisation drew heavily from Egyptian and Phoenician sources, and that this debt was systematically erased by Eurocentric scholarship.

Academic Sources

  • Ober, J. — "Athenian Democracy: A Brief Overview" (multiple scholarly volumes). On democracy's mechanisms and exclusions.
  • Scholarly articles on slavery in Athens — evidence that ~30–40% of the population were enslaved, and that Athenian democracy's rise coincided with the expansion of slavery.

Key Debate: The 'Greek Miracle' — Original Achievement or Built on African and Near Eastern Foundations?

Position A — The Greek Miracle (Conventional view): Greek civilisation — philosophy, democracy, rhetoric, rational inquiry — represented a revolutionary departure from 'Oriental' traditions. Greece was the source of the Western intellectual tradition.

Position B — Bernal's Black Athena: Greek culture was heavily indebted to Egypt and Phoenicia. The 'Aryan model' (claiming Greek cultural independence) replaced an older 'Ancient Model' in which Greeks themselves acknowledged their debts to Egyptian and Eastern predecessors. Eurocentric 18th–19th century scholarship rewrote this history.

The Scholarly Response to Bernal: Many classicists have criticised Bernal's specific claims (etymology, archaeological dating) while acknowledging his larger point: Greek civilisation did borrow extensively from the Near East and Egypt, and this was obscured by Eurocentric scholarship. The debate has been productive even where Bernal's evidence is weak.

Discussion Prompts

  1. Josiah Ober argues democracy was revolutionary for those included. Is 'revolutionary' still the right word when 60–70% of the population were excluded or enslaved?
  2. Martin Bernal's Black Athena thesis was attacked on both scholarly and political grounds. How do you evaluate a historical argument when the academic debate and the political debate are so intertwined?
  3. Sparta and Athens are often presented as opposites. Both relied on enslaved labour. Does this shared foundation undermine the usual 'democracy vs. militarism' framing?
  4. Is the concept of the 'Greek miracle' a historical observation or a historiographical construct?

Additional Deep Dive

  • Democracy and Knowledge — Josiah Ober (Princeton, 2008).
  • Greece in the Making, 1200–479 BC — Robin Osborne (Routledge, 1996).
  • The Ancient Greeks — Moses I. Finley (Penguin, 1977).

Module 4: The Roman Republic and Empire

Timeframe: c. 753 BCE – 476 CE

Overview

Rome is the longest-running story in this course — over a thousand years from the founding of the Republic to the fall of the Western Empire. It is also one of the most interpreted. Every generation since the Renaissance has used Rome as a mirror: republicans, imperialists, revolutionaries, and fascists have all claimed its legacy. This module focuses on two central questions: why did the Republic fall? And did the Empire 'fall' — or transform?

Core Readings

SPQR: A History of Ancient Rome — Mary Beard (Liveright, 2015). The best accessible scholarly account of Rome; Beard is a Cambridge classicist who pays particular attention to who was excluded from Roman history.
As the Romans Did: A Sourcebook in Roman Social History — Joann Shelton (2nd ed., Oxford University Press, 1997). Primary source anthology: slavery, women, household life, law, gladiators — hearing Roman voices directly.
The Fall of Rome and the End of Civilization — Bryan Ward-Perkins (Oxford University Press, 2005). Argues, against the fashionable 'transformation' school, that the 5th century was genuinely catastrophic — real economic and cultural collapse, not peaceful change.

Academic Sources

  • Jones, A.H.M. — chapters from The Later Roman Empire (Johns Hopkins, 1964) on Roman law, slavery, and provincial administration. Still the standard reference after 60 years.
  • Harper, K. — key arguments from The Fate of Rome: Climate, Disease, and the End of an Empire (Princeton, 2017) — integrating paleoclimatology and epidemiology.

The History of Rome Podcast History of Rome – First Episode on Spotify The History of Rome – Internet Archive (Full) The Ancients Podcast – Fall of Rome MIT OCW – Ancient World: Rome Gibbon's Decline and Fall – Vol. 1 (Project Gutenberg)

Key Debate: Did Rome 'Fall' or Transform?

Position A — Decline and Fall (Gibbon; also Ward-Perkins): Rome fell in 476 CE. The Western Empire collapsed under barbarian pressure. Standards of living dropped measurably: pottery quality declined, coin circulation fell, literacy collapsed, urban populations shrank. Ward-Perkins uses archaeological evidence to demonstrate that there was a real 'end of civilisation' in the West — not a peaceful transformation.

Position B — Transformation (Peter Brown and the 'Late Antiquity' School): The 5th–7th centuries were not a catastrophic collapse but a cultural transformation. Christianity, legal traditions, and urban life persisted in new forms. 'Late Antiquity' was creatively vibrant, not dark. The periodisation 'fall of Rome' is a later intellectual construction.

Position C — Environmental/Biological Factors (Kyle Harper, The Fate of Rome, 2017): Climate change (volcanic eruptions, solar cycles, cooling temperatures from the 3rd century onward) and epidemic disease (the Antonine Plague, the Plague of Cyprian, the Plague of Justinian) were primary drivers of demographic and economic decline — factors largely beyond Rome's political control.

Discussion Prompts

  1. Was the fall of the Roman Republic structurally inevitable, or was it contingent on the choices of individuals like Caesar, Pompey, and Augustus?
  2. Mary Beard argues asking 'why did Rome fall' is the wrong question. Do you agree? What would be the better question?
  3. Bryan Ward-Perkins and Peter Brown are both serious scholars who reach opposite conclusions about the 5th century. What does this tell you about how historical evidence works?
  4. Kyle Harper introduces climate and disease as historical agents. Does this kind of structural/environmental explanation reduce or enhance your understanding of Roman history?

Additional Deep Dive

  • The Fate of Rome: Climate, Disease, and the End of an Empire — Kyle Harper (Princeton, 2017).
  • The Rise of Western Christendom — Peter Brown (2nd ed., Blackwell, 2003).
  • The History of the Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire — Edward Gibbon (1776–1788; abridged Penguin edition, 1985). Read Gibbon not for accuracy but because understanding historiography means reading the texts that shaped it.

Module 5: Connections and Collapse — Trade, Religion, and the Late Antique World

Timeframe: c. 200–800 CE

Overview

This module asks you to zoom out and see the ancient Mediterranean not as a series of separate civilisations but as a connected system — of trade, religion, disease, and ideas. It also asks you to question one of the most influential theses in medieval history: Henri Pirenne's argument that it was Islamic expansion, not the Germanic invasions, that truly severed the Mediterranean world and created the Middle Ages.

Core Readings

The Rise of Western Christendom — Peter Brown (2nd ed., Blackwell, 2003). Definitive history of Christianity's expansion across the former Roman world; argues for vitality, not decline.
The Fate of Rome: Climate, Disease, and the End of an Empire — Kyle Harper (Princeton, 2017). Groundbreaking integration of paleoclimatology, epidemiology, and history.
The Fall of Rome and the End of Civilization — Bryan Ward-Perkins (Oxford, 2005). Counterargument: uses archaeological evidence (pottery, coinage, building) to demonstrate real economic collapse.

Academic Sources

  • Pirenne, H. — key arguments from Mohammed and Charlemagne (1939): Islamic conquests, not Germanic invasions, severed Mediterranean trade.
  • Effros, B. — "The Enduring Attraction of the Pirenne Thesis" — critical examination of Pirenne's evidence and its limits.

Key Debate: The Pirenne Thesis — Did Islam Create the Middle Ages?

Position A — Pirenne's Thesis (1939): It was the Islamic conquests of the 7th century — not the Germanic invasions of the 5th — that truly ended the ancient Mediterranean world. By cutting off western Europe from eastern trade networks, Islam forced northern Europe to become self-sufficient, creating the conditions for the Carolingian world and ultimately medieval Europe.

Position B — The Critique: Archaeological evidence (later examined by Maurice Lombard and others) shows that Mediterranean trade continued through and after the Islamic conquests, albeit in transformed forms. Muslim merchants were active traders; the Mediterranean remained a zone of exchange. The break was less clean and less catastrophic than Pirenne claimed.

Position C — Environmental/Pandemic Explanation (Harper): The real discontinuity was not political (Germanic invasions or Islamic conquests) but biological and climatic: the Plague of Justinian (541–549 CE), possibly the first bubonic plague pandemic, killed 25–50 million people across the Mediterranean and Near East. Combined with climate cooling, this reshaped demographics, trade, and political power far more than any military event.

Discussion Prompts

  1. Pirenne wrote Mohammed and Charlemagne while imprisoned by the Germans in World War I, working from memory. Does this context — both the biographical and the geopolitical — affect how you read his argument?
  2. What would it feel like to live through the 'end' of the ancient world? What evidence would a person living in, say, 500 CE have that something had fundamentally changed?
  3. Is 'Late Antiquity' a useful periodisation, or does it obscure more than it reveals by avoiding the word 'decline'?
  4. You have now studied Mesopotamia, Egypt, Greece, and Rome. What single structural feature — political, economic, ecological — connects all four civilisations?

Additional Deep Dive

  • Augustine of Hippo: A Biography — Peter Brown (2000). Intellectual history of late antiquity through its greatest thinker.
  • The Later Roman Empire — Averil Cameron (Fontana Press, 1993).
  • Coinage in the Roman Economy — Kenneth Harl (Johns Hopkins, 1996). For the economic continuity/decline debate.
